Scoring

Make 0–0–2 make sense.

Traditional doubles scoring uses three numbers. Use the simulator below to see how the serve changes after each rally.

Traditional doubles practice

0 Serving
Server 2
0 Receiving
Call: 0 – 0 – 2

What the three numbers mean

  1. First: serving team's score.
  2. Second: receiving team's score.
  3. Third: server 1 or server 2.
Example: 6–4–2
The serving team has 6, the receiving team has 4, and server 2 is serving.

Important scoring rules

  1. Only the serving team scores under traditional scoring.
  2. If server 1 loses, the partner becomes server 2.
  3. If server 2 loses, the other team gets the serve.
  4. The game begins at 0–0–2 because the first team receives only one service turn.
Guided lesson

Learn the game one step at a time.

Move forward only when each rule makes sense. Each step now includes a visual so learners can actually see what the rule means.

Step 1 of 10
1
Set up the game

Choose singles or doubles.

Singles has one player on each side. Doubles has two players on each side and is the most common format for beginners.

For your first game, doubles is usually easier and more social.
2
Choose the first server

Start from the right side.

Use a coin toss, paddle spin, or another fair method. The first server stands behind the baseline on the right side of the court.

Right side first. Stay behind the baseline when serving.
3
Call the score

In doubles, begin with 0–0–2.

The first number is the serving team’s score, the second is the receiving team’s score, and the third identifies server one or server two.

The opening team starts as server two because it gets only one service turn.
4
Serve correctly

Serve diagonally across the court.

Send the ball into the opposite service box. It must clear the kitchen and land beyond the kitchen line.

Behind the baseline. Across the court. Beyond the kitchen.
5
The two-bounce rule

Let the ball bounce once on each side.

The receiving team lets the serve bounce. The serving team then lets the return bounce. After those two required bounces, open play begins.

Serve → bounce → return → bounce → open play.
6
Volleying

A volley is a hit before the ball bounces.

Once the two-bounce rule is complete, you may hit the ball in the air. This is called a volley.

You cannot volley during the first two required bounces.
7
The kitchen

Do not volley while touching the kitchen.

The kitchen is the seven-foot non-volley zone beside the net. You may enter it and hit a ball after it bounces, but you may not volley while touching it or its line.

The kitchen is a no-volley zone, not a no-entry zone.
8
Kitchen momentum

Stay balanced after a volley.

If you volley outside the kitchen but your momentum carries you into it, the shot is still a fault.

Finish your volley without stepping on the kitchen line or into the kitchen.
9
Faults and line calls

Know when the rally ends.

Common faults include hitting out, hitting into the net, allowing two bounces, touching the net, or breaking a kitchen rule. Boundary lines are in, but a serve touching the kitchen line is a fault.

When you are unsure whether a ball is out, call it in.
10
Score and win

Only the serving team scores.

Under traditional scoring, win a rally while serving to earn a point. Games are usually played to 11, and you must win by at least two points.

You now know enough to play: serve, bounce twice, respect the kitchen, and win by two.
